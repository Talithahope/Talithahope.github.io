About me

Bridging environmental science with human experience

I'm a human-environment researcher in the School of Geography, Development, and Environment at the University of Arizona. My work sits at the intersection of climate science, agriculture, and the lived experiences of farming communities.

I believe the challenges reshaping agriculture, from rising temperatures and water scarcity to threats to worker health and food quality, cannot be fully understood or solved in isolation. That's why my research deliberately bridges mathematical modelling, field-based environmental measurements, laboratory analysis, and qualitative fieldwork.

Whether I'm calibrating sensors in 38°C desert heat, sitting with farmworkers to understand their daily realities, or mapping climate projections through 2080, my goal is the same: ensuring that climate adaptation strategies respond to the needs of the communities they're meant to serve.

PhD Research

Agrivoltaics as climate adaptation

Programme
Major: Geography  |  Minors: Geospatial Information Technologies & Global Change

My dissertation focuses on agrivoltaics, the integration of agriculture under and around solar panels, as a practical climate adaptation strategy. I ask whether a single intervention can simultaneously sustain agricultural production, advance renewable energy deployment, and protect the health of farmworkers.

Agrivoltaic systems can shade and cool the environment beneath them, reducing moisture loss and directly countering several pressures climate change places on agriculture. My research examines this from three interconnected angles:

01

Where should agrivoltaics go?

Drawing on field trials and large-scale geographic and climate data, I developed spatial models identifying where agrivoltaic systems would most effectively reduce water loss, buffer crops against extreme heat, and sustain productivity across the American Southwest. Because climate change is dynamic, my models evaluate present and future scenarios through 2080, mapping how optimal locations evolve through the century.

Spatial MCDA Climate Projections GIS
02

Can they protect farmworkers?

Farmworkers face heat-related mortality risks 20 to 35 times higher than indoor workers. Over three years, I measured heat stress on adjacent agrivoltaic and conventional farms, producing the first evidence that agrivoltaic systems can meaningfully reduce the heat burden farmworkers face. I pair sensor data with in-depth farmworker interviews, listening to how they experience heat across the working day.

Microclimate Worker Health Participant Observation
03

What happens to the food?

The same environmental stresses that endanger farmworkers also threaten the nutritional quality of crops. Across three years of field trials, I processed hundreds of samples from crops grown under both agrivoltaic and conventional conditions, analyzing changes in inorganic nutrient content including iron, zinc, and manganese.

Science Communication

Beyond the journal article

Research only matters if it reaches the people who can use it. I care deeply about finding ways to share knowledge that go beyond traditional academic publishing, whether that's writing first-person essays, presenting to farming communities, speaking at conferences, or co-authoring practical guides for practitioners.

I try to ground my communication in storytelling: connecting research findings to everyday experiences, linking headlines about deadly heat waves to what I see in the field, and centring the human stories behind the data. The goal is always to make complex climate and agricultural research feel both understandable and urgent.
