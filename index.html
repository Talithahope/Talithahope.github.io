About My Research

I'm a 3rd year PhD candidate in the School of Geography, Development, and Environment at the University of Arizona, investigating how agrivoltaic systems can serve as comprehensive climate adaptation strategies for agricultural communities. My work examines the interconnected benefits of combining solar energy with farming, from protecting farmworker health through heat mitigation to enhancing crop productivity and environmental sustainability in the face of climate change.

Climate Adaptation Agrivoltaics Systems thinking

Research Approach

My interdisciplinary methodology bridges physical and human geography through mixed-methods research. I combine microclimate monitoring and spatial modeling to quantify environmental changes, while using semi-structured interviews and participatory research to understand farmer perspectives and adaptation experiences. This human-environment approach allows me to assess both the measurable impacts of agrivoltaic systems and the lived experiences of agricultural communities adapting to climate change.

Mixed methods Empirical Analysis Qualitative Interviews

Background

Originally from a small town in the Lake District, England, I hold degrees from the University of Sheffield in Environmental Change & International Development (MSc) and Physical Geography (BSc). My specialization in climate change and agriculture began during my master's degree with research on farmers' perceptions of and adaptations to climate change in Sierra Leone and agrivoltaic spatial modeling in East Africa, establishing my focus on bridging scientific analysis with human perspectives in agricultural climate adaptation.

Research Projects

Agrivoltaic Spatial Suitability Modeling

I use spatial analysis to identify optimal locations for agrivoltaic deployment across Great Britain, the Southwestern U.S., and East Africa. My approach employs multi-criteria decision analysis to assess technical feasibility, with published research including current suitability mapping for Great Britain and climate-informed projections for the Southwestern U.S. through 2080. This work began during my master's degree with participatory modeling in East Africa conducted in partnership with the World Agroforestry Center, which integrated stakeholder engagement through collaborative workshops to understand community priorities and define suitable use cases for different agricultural contexts.

Farmworker Heat Stress Mitigation

I investigate how agrivoltaic systems can protect agricultural workers from heat exposure using both environmental data and worker experiences. My research combines microclimate monitoring at agrivoltaic and traditional field sites to quantify thermal conditions, with semi-structured interviews and participant observation to understand how farmworkers experience working under solar panels. This approach bridges quantitative measurements of heat mitigation with qualitative insights into occupational health impacts, providing a comprehensive assessment of agrivoltaics as a worker protection strategy.

Science Communication

Making Science Accessible

I believe science is only truly valuable when it can be communicated effectively to diverse audiences. My approach focuses on storytelling and connecting research findings to real-world impacts and human experiences. I work to ground academic research in everyday realities by linking news headlines about deadly heat waves to my research on farmworker protection, explaining how climate projections translate to local farming challenges, and highlighting the human stories behind the data. From presenting to international conferences and engaging with policymakers to speaking with community groups and co-authoring practical resources like the Best Practices in Agrisolar guide, I strive to make complex climate and agricultural research both understandable and compelling.

Research Group: I'm part of the Barron-Gafford Research Group at the University of Arizona, led by my supervisor Dr. Greg Barron-Gafford.
